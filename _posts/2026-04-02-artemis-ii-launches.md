Yesterday afternoon, at US Eastern Time, the NASA's Artemis II mission commenced, launched by the
[Space Launch System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Launch_System) (SLS) rocket from the
Kennedy Space Center and heading for a flyby of the moon. As virtually all of the media commentary 
notes, this will be the first time in 53 years that a manned mission has gone beyond the 
[low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit) zone to which all manned "space" flight 
has been confined since Apollo 17 ended. Artemis II is set to break records for how
far from Earth a manned mission will go, and is envisioned as setting the ground for a future moon 
landing which will in turn pave the way for the ultimate destination of [Mars](https://www.nasa.gov/humans-in-space/artemis/).

Having grown up imbibing science-fiction and with the Apollo program as a fixture in the recent historical
background, it would be natural (perhaps even expected) for me to feel excitement at the progress of the 
Artemis program, but I have to confess to only tepid interest. While I certainly wish the crew of Artemis a safe,
trouble-free trip and return, I don't see how anything in this or the next two Artemis missions will contribute 
much to scientific understanding. Going back to the moon will teach us nothing new about the effects of 
spaceflight on the human body, while the burden of packing all of the food and life-support systems needed to
keep a human crew alive and well means there's that much less room to include any scientific instruments which
could teach us something we haven't already gleaned from any number of unmanned space missions over the last 
few decades.

The only part of the Artemis program which stands to provide any new scientific understanding is the last and (by
a considerable distance) hardest stage, which entails sending a manned crew all the way to Mars. Sending human 
beings to the moon and back was so ambitious an undertaking that only one nation has ever mustered the resources
to do so, and no one even dreaming of trying in the many decades since the USA stepped back from the venture in 1972. 
The Earth-Moon distance is approximately **361,000km** when the two at the two bodies' closest, or about **28 times** the
diameter of the Earth. As gargantuan as this distance is, however, it is almost **nothing** compared to the distance
from Earth to Mars: ~**54,600,000km** at closest approach, or more than **150 times** greater than the Earth-Moon distance.
Worse yet, Earth and Mars aren't actually set to be so close for the next **262 years** (i.e. until the year 2287). 
In 2029, by which time the Artemis moon landings are currently planned to have occurred, the closest Earth and Mars
will get is 97,240,000km, or ~**270 times** the minimum Earth-Moon distance.

Given just how much further away Mars is at the best of times, any manned space mission to the red planet will require 
a one-way trip time on the order of several months, with a trip carrying enough supplies to keep the crew alive for
the entirety of that journey. On the realistic assumption that the crew are unable to secure much in the way of supplies
on Mars itself, the outbound mission will need to have enough supplies for both legs of the journey. Needless to say, some 
level of safety margin will also need to be accounted, as a voyage of such length is unlikely to be entirely smooth sailing.
It is doubtful that any currently feasible launch system is capable of launching everything required all in one go. 

As onerous as all of the preceding might sound, it only scratches the surface of the difficulties a Martian expedition would
entail, as there's also the question of [radiation](https://www.scientificamerican.com/blog/life-unbounded/death-on-mars1/) [exposure](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.340.6136.1031) to deal with. The US Department of Energy (DoE) sets a maximum annual radiation exposure 
limit of 20 millisieverts (mSv). A stay of 6 months on the ISS exposes astronauts to about 75 mSv, or nearly **4 times** the DoE limit. 
A round-trip to Mars, however, would likely expose travellers to about 662 mSv, or more than **33 times** the DoE limit! NASA
is certainly [actively wrestling with](https://www.science.org/content/article/humans-return-moon-scientists-confront-dangers-deep-space-radiation) 
this problem, but even if effective countermeasures are found, they are likely to inflate the cost and weight of a manned Mars
mission even further than any simple extrapolation from Moon landings would imply. 

I'm not going into all of this detail because I have any sort of innate aversion to the idea of manned space travel as such, far from it.
Like nearly everyone else who grew up watching Star Wars, Star Trek and innumerable other such movies and TV shows, the idea of humans in
space seemed to me not just desirable but almost inevitable, with expeditions to Mars a natural next step after going to the Moon. Had the 
state of my knowledge remained as it was in childhood, I might still be taken in by charlatans peddling fantasies of Martian colonies, but 
luckily, along the way to adulthood, I got to pick up much of the wisdom to now be found neatly assembled in books like the 
Weinersmiths' [A City on Mars](https://www.acityonmars.com/), which I cannot recommend highly enough. 

But even as I was learning about the many difficulties which lay in the way of translating science-fiction dreams into reality, 
revelations continued to flood in from the many unmanned NASA missions which were launched in the intervening decades, from the
[Voyager missions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_program) in the 1970s, through the [Cassini-Huygens mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens) in the 1990s, to the [Juno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juno_(spacecraft)), 
[New Horizons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Horizons) and [Mars 2020](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_2020) missions since. 
All of these unmanned missions have provided many magnitudes more scientifically valuable data than the manned missions ever have, and all
have done so at far much less cost. In fact, the data collected by these missions continues to provide new insights long after the missions
themselves have concluded, with new papers being published about even the Voyager data to the present day. 

It's true enough that unmanned missions don't provide the same kinds of visual imagery that accompanies astronauts leaping around on
the surface of the moon, but the sorts of imagery they can provide is often far more spectacular than anything creatures of flesh and
blood could ever take. No human being could withstand the fierce [radiation surrounding Jupiter](https://www.esa.int/Enabling_Support/Space_Engineering_Technology/Jupiter_s_radiation_belts_and_how_to_survive_them) long enough to capture the kinds of 
[magnificent pictures](https://www.planetary.org/articles/best-jupiter-pictures-nasa-juno-mission) taken by the Juno spacecraft, for example.
Meanwhile, even on (relatively) close and benign Mars, the [Perseverance rover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseverance_(rover)) has been 
taking detailed pictures of the Martian surface for the last 5 years, under a level of accumulated radiation exposure which would have killed 
any human being able to withstand the extremes of Martian temperature.

The long and short of it is that manned space travel makes no sense from either a scientific or an economic point of view. It is best considered
as a form of political theatre, providing a stage for superpowers like the United States and China to show off their economic might and engineering
prowess to the rest of the world. In as far as it serves as a substitute for older and more violent forms of superpower competition, I suppose this 
particular contest should be considered benign, but as far as actual science goes, the bits that are feasible[^1] aren't interesting, while the bits 
that _are_ of even slight scientific interest[^2] aren't currently feasible.

[^1]: The "going to the Moon" part.

[^2]: The "going to Mars" portion.
