Back in August last year, I [laid out](https://alapite.github.io/mathematics/2025/08/14/questions-of-magnitude.html) my belief that mathematical notation ought to be considered as a technology in its own right, one that both makes well-established operations easier and enables new conceptions which had hitherto been beyond reach. In my post, I used the adoption of [Arabic numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_numerals) as a key example of this process, showing how they made easy operations which the Romans, the Greeks and earlier peoples had long struggled with. The Arabic numerals in turn opened the way for the creation of [scientific notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_notation), which has proven so powerful that it can easily handle any numbers relevant to the physical world, from the [Planck scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_units) right up to the entire [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe). Any high-school student who has mastered scientific notation can confidently manipulate quantities only the very greatest minds of antiquity could even [have conceived](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Sand_Reckoner). 

I'm bringing all of this up again now because Quanta Magazine has just [published an article](https://www.quantamagazine.org/how-writing-changes-mathematical-thought-20260325/) along the very same lines, describing the work of David E. Dunning, a historian at the Smithsonian who studies _"the social effects of notation"_. As I did in my original post, Dunning explains how the cumbersome Roman numeric system hindered the ability to carry out arithmetical processes which would be considered quite basic today. Dunning also brings up the same point I made about the expressive limits of the Roman numeral system.
>With Roman numerals, you need a new symbol every time you reach a new order of magnitude. 

Arabic numerals cast aside these limitations, making possible the tremendous advances in science and engineering which have brought us to the modern world. 

In my own post, I kept the focus purely on the question of magnitudes and their representation, but in the Quanta article, Dunning goes beyond this to discussing the invention of the calculus, and the impact of the different notational choices made by Leibniz and Newton. Even though the two men were working with the same concepts, the [notation invented by Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz%27s_notation) offered such compelling advantages that [Newton's alternative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Notation_for_differentiation#Newton's_notation) is mostly relegated to the field of physics (and even there, its use is restricted to derivatives). 

The advantages of Leibniz notation are already obvious when dealing with techniques like the [chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule), but it is when one ventures beyond elementary calculus into the world of [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form) that the benefits of Leibniz' system become truly apparent. It's hard to imagine modern differential geometry being where it is without this foundation, and it's even harder to imagine Einstein successfully formalising general relativity without being able to work with the tools provided by differential geometry.

One last aspect that Dunning touches on that I think deserves mention is his discussion of the lack of a standard notational system in the field of mathematical logic, and how that set the arena for the groundbreaking work done by Alonzo Church and Alan Turing in the 1930s. In this regard, I think it's also worth noting Kurt Gödel's closely related work in the same period. The [Halting Problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halting_problem) and [Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del%27s_incompleteness_theorem) can be considered as variant takes on the same underlying question, namely: **To what extent can thought be captured by a notational system?** As all of the 1930s work shows, the answer is "imperfectly". There can never be a notational system so powerful that its wielders can answer any possible question simply by saying ["Let us calculate"](https://spectrum.ieee.org/in-the-17th-century-leibniz-dreamed-of-a-machine-that-could-calculate-ideas).

As pessimistic as this conclusion might appear, we need not draw the wrong lesson from it. Just because no single, totally powerful notation system is possible in principle doesn't mean foreclose the possibility of incrementally improving on any notational systems we may currently be using, any more than the impossibility of reaching infinity means we can't find a successor for any particular whole number we are given. If we accept that the lifetime[^1] of the observable universe is finite, and the lifetime of any species within it is even more definitive in its finitude, then it's clear that the sum total of mathematical ideas that the entire human species can ever have is also finite, however unimaginably large that sum might be. Given this reality, it's quite reasonable to suppose that there's some particular Turing Machine which can give authoritative answers to any and all questions which any human being might ever wish to ask. Of course, there's no particular reason to believe such a machine would be within the grasp of any particular human, whether living or yet to be born ...

Veering away from the world of science fiction, knowing that the notational systems we employ will always be imperfect, we have every incentive to improve upon them, and to reiterate the process as we come to the limits of the latest round of improvements (just as we do with any other technology). To give a concrete example, I mentioned in my August post that the [Knuth up-arrow notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knuth%27s_up-arrow_notation) was nowhere close to being as far as one could go in the direction of handling larger magnitudes. I intend to return to this topic in an upcoming post, in which I will illustrate what is made possible by yet more powerful systems for representing finite numbers.   

[^1]: Where by "lifetime" I mean the period until it reaches the stage of [heat death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_death_of_the_universe).
